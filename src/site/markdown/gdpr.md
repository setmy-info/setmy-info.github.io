# GDPR

## Information

**GDPR** (General Data Protection Regulation) is EU Regulation 2016/679. It entered into force on 25 May 2018 and
applies to any organisation — regardless of location — that processes personal data of individuals in the EU/EEA.

### Key Principles (Article 5)

* **Lawfulness, fairness and transparency** — processing must have a legal basis and be transparent to the data subject.
* **Purpose limitation** — data collected for specified, explicit, legitimate purposes; not further processed incompatibly.
* **Data minimisation** — only data adequate and necessary for the purpose may be collected.
* **Accuracy** — data must be kept accurate and up to date.
* **Storage limitation** — data must not be kept longer than necessary.
* **Integrity and confidentiality** — appropriate security measures must protect the data.
* **Accountability** — the controller is responsible for demonstrating compliance.

### Legal Bases for Processing (Article 6)

* Consent
* Contract performance
* Legal obligation
* Vital interests
* Public task
* Legitimate interests (subject to balancing test)

### Key Rights of Data Subjects

* **Right of access** (Article 15) — individuals can request a copy of their data.
* **Right to rectification** (Article 16) — correction of inaccurate data.
* **Right to erasure / right to be forgotten** (Article 17) — deletion under certain conditions.
* **Right to data portability** (Article 20) — receive data in machine-readable format.
* **Right to object** (Article 21) — object to processing based on legitimate interests or for direct marketing.
* **Rights related to automated decision-making** (Article 22) — not to be subject to solely automated decisions with
  significant effects.

### Data Protection Officer (DPO)

Mandatory for public authorities, organisations that carry out large-scale systematic monitoring, or large-scale
processing of special categories of data. The DPO must be independent and report to the highest management level.

### Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA)

Required before processing that is likely to result in high risk to individuals (Article 35). Typical triggers:
systematic profiling, large-scale processing of sensitive data, large-scale use of new technology.

### Data Breach Notification

* Notify supervisory authority within **72 hours** of becoming aware (Article 33).
* Notify affected individuals without undue delay if the breach is likely to result in high risk (Article 34).

## Estonian GDPR

### Supervisory Authority

The **AKI** (Andmekaitse Inspektsioon / Data Protection Inspectorate) is Estonia's supervisory authority responsible
for GDPR enforcement and guidance.

### National Law

The **IKS** (Isikuandmete kaitse seadus — Personal Data Protection Act) implements GDPR in Estonian national law and
covers areas where the regulation allows member-state specification (e.g. employment context, public interest
processing).

### Estonian e-Resident Context

Processing personal data of e-residents is subject to the same GDPR rules as for any other EU data subject. The
digital identity framework (ID-card, Mobile-ID, Smart-ID) is commonly used for authentication and consent purposes in
Estonian digital services.

## Usage, tips and tricks

* Document all processing activities in a **Record of Processing Activities (RoPA)** (Article 30).
* Implement **privacy by design and by default** (Article 25) — build privacy measures into systems from the start.
* Use **data minimisation** checklists when designing forms and APIs.
* Review third-party **Data Processing Agreements (DPAs)** before sharing data with processors.
* Conduct **DPIA** before launching high-risk processing activities.
