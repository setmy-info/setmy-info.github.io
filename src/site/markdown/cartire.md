# Car Tires

## Information

Car tire sizing uses a standardized notation printed on the tire sidewall. Understanding the notation is important for
choosing a safe replacement tire with the correct load capacity and speed rating for your vehicle.

Where to find your required tire specification:

* Door jamb placard (sticker inside the driver's door frame)
* Owner's manual
* Existing tires on the vehicle

## Tire Size and Load/Speed Rating Explained

**215/65R16 98H — meaning**

**215** — Tire width in millimeters (215 mm)

**65** — Aspect ratio: sidewall height is 65% of the tire width

**R** — Tire construction type: radial

**16** — Rim diameter in inches (16")

**98** — Load index (maximum load per tire: approx. 750 kg)

**H** — Speed index (maximum approved speed: up to 210 km/h)

### Load Index — common values

| Load index | Max load per tire |
|------------|-------------------|
| 88         | 560 kg            |
| 91         | 615 kg            |
| 94         | 670 kg            |
| 95         | 690 kg            |
| 97         | 730 kg            |
| 98         | 750 kg            |
| 100        | 800 kg            |
| 103        | 875 kg            |
| 106        | 950 kg            |

Always use a load index equal to or higher than the manufacturer's specification. Never downgrade load index.

### Speed Index — common values

| Speed index | Max speed  |
|-------------|------------|
| T           | 190 km/h   |
| H           | 210 km/h   |
| V           | 240 km/h   |
| W           | 270 km/h   |
| Y           | 300 km/h   |
| ZR          | > 240 km/h |

Always use a speed index equal to or higher than the manufacturer's specification.

## Usage, tips and tricks

* Winter tires may have a lower speed rating (e.g. T or H). This is acceptable as long as you drive within the rated
  speed — most countries permit it for seasonal winter tires.
* Mixing tire sizes or load ratings on the same axle is unsafe and often illegal.
* After replacing tires, recalibrate the TPMS (Tire Pressure Monitoring System) if your vehicle has one.
* Tire age matters: most manufacturers recommend replacing tires after 6–10 years regardless of tread depth.
