# Effort–Impact Matrix

## Information

The Effort–Impact Matrix is a prioritisation tool that helps teams decide which tasks or initiatives to tackle first
by mapping them against two axes: how much effort they require and what impact they will have.

It is conceptually related to the Eisenhower Matrix but is used for project and backlog prioritisation rather than
personal time management. Teams use it to make explicit, shared decisions about where to focus limited capacity.

### Quadrants

|                   | **High Impact**                           | **Medium Impact**              | **Low Impact**              |
|-------------------|-------------------------------------------|--------------------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Low Effort**    | Quick Wins — do first                     | Easy Gains — batch them        | Small extras — only if idle |
| **Medium Effort** | Strategic Bets — plan and schedule        | Worth Considering — evaluate   | Maybe later — deprioritise  |
| **High Effort**   | Big Projects — only if ROI is clear       | Doubtful — challenge the value | Time Wasters — drop/defer   |

* **Quick Wins** (high impact, low effort) — highest priority. Do these immediately. They deliver disproportionate
  value for the cost.
* **Strategic Bets** (high impact, high effort) — worth doing but require planning, resourcing, and a clear timeline.
  Do not start without commitment.
* **Easy Gains** (medium impact, low effort) — useful but not urgent. Batch them into a sprint when capacity allows.
* **Time Wasters** (low impact, high effort) — drop or defer. The cost outweighs the return.

## Usage, tips and tricks

### Running a Prioritisation Session

1. Collect the candidate items (backlog tasks, initiatives, improvement ideas) on sticky notes or a shared board.
2. For each item, the team scores two dimensions independently:
   * **Effort** — 1 (trivial) to 5 (very large). Consider time, complexity, dependencies, and risk.
   * **Impact** — 1 (negligible) to 5 (transformative). Consider business value, user benefit, or risk reduction.
3. Place items on the matrix grid based on their scores.
4. Review placements together. Items close to quadrant borders often need discussion.
5. Agree on the top Quick Wins and start there. Schedule Strategic Bets. Drop Time Wasters.

### Tips

* Keep sessions time-boxed (30–60 minutes). Perfect scoring is less important than shared understanding.
* Revisit the matrix regularly — effort and impact estimates change as the project evolves.
* Use the matrix for relative comparison, not absolute planning — it reveals priorities, not timelines.
* Avoid the trap of only doing Quick Wins: Strategic Bets often drive the most long-term value.
