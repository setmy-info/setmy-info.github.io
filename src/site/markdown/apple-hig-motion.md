# Apple Human Interface Guidelines Motion

## Information

`Apple Human Interface Guidelines Motion` is Apple’s design guidance for using animation and movement to support
clarity,
feedback, hierarchy, and continuity across Apple platform interfaces.

In practice, this guidance helps product teams treat motion as a usability tool rather than decoration. It explains how
transitions, layered movement, and animated feedback can make interfaces feel more understandable, spatially coherent,
and responsive to user actions.

It is especially relevant when teams design applications for `iOS`, `iPadOS`, `macOS`, or other Apple platforms and
want motion behavior that feels native to the platform.

### Main Functionalities and Features

* **Continuity Guidance**: Encourages transitions that help users understand how views, elements, and states relate.
* **Feedback Through Motion**: Uses animation to confirm actions, changes, and system response.
* **Spatial Hierarchy**: Supports layered interfaces where movement reinforces depth, focus, and navigation flow.
* **Platform-Native Behavior**: Aligns motion with the feel and interaction expectations of Apple ecosystems.
* **Restraint and Clarity**: Promotes purposeful animation over decorative excess.

### How Apple HIG Motion Fits Into Product Design

`Apple Human Interface Guidelines Motion` is not an animation library. It is motion design guidance.

It is often used when:

* a team wants motion to feel native on Apple platforms,
* interface transitions should communicate navigation and hierarchy,
* or designers need shared principles for feedback, layering, and movement.

### Common Use Cases

* Design screen transitions that preserve context during navigation.
* Add subtle feedback to gestures, confirmations, and state changes.
* Improve perceived quality and platform consistency in Apple-focused apps.
* Align product motion rules with a broader Apple design-system approach.

### Practical Notes

* Apple guidance generally favors motion that explains interface change rather than drawing attention to itself.
* Excessive animation, delayed response, or overly playful movement can work against clarity and perceived performance.
* Teams should also consider accessibility preferences such as reduced motion.
