# Page Flows

## Information

`Page Flows` is a UI and UX research resource that curates recorded user flows, screens, and interaction patterns from
real digital products.

In practice, teams use `Page Flows` as a reference source for benchmarking onboarding, signup, checkout, search,
navigation, and other product journeys across well-known applications. It is commonly used for inspiration, competitive
analysis, and UX pattern review.

It is especially relevant when product teams want to study how real products structure user journeys rather than only
looking at isolated mockups or static component galleries.

### Main Functionalities and Features

* **Recorded User Flows**: Shows complete journeys from real apps and websites.
* **UX Pattern Research**: Helps teams compare common approaches to navigation, onboarding, retention, and conversion.
* **Inspiration Library**: Useful for discovering practical interaction ideas across products.
* **Journey-Level Perspective**: Focuses on end-to-end flows instead of only single screens.
* **Benchmarking Support**: Often used in product discovery and UX review work.

### How Page Flows Fits Into Design Work

`Page Flows` is not a design system or component library. It is a UX research and inspiration resource.

It is often used when:

* a team is designing or improving product journeys,
* UX researchers want examples of how other products handle a flow,
* or product teams need practical references before defining their own interaction patterns.

### Common Use Cases

* Research onboarding and signup flows before designing a new product experience.
* Compare checkout, account, or retention patterns across products.
* Support workshops, audits, or redesign discussions with real examples.
* Inspire flow-level decisions beyond isolated component styling.

### Practical Notes

* `Page Flows` is best used as reference material, not as a source to copy patterns blindly.
* Strong design decisions still need product context, accessibility review, and user validation.
* Teams should study the intent behind a flow, not only its visual surface.
